The quote of the week for April 13 - 17 is directly from Winston Churchill, and is as follows: “Success is not final, failure is not fatal: it is the courage to continue that counts.” This statement widely attributes the truth of what progression looks like in our lives. Unlike the naive belief that if you do not immediately achieve the goals you set, you’ve effectively done something wrong, this claim is refuted by what we all experience, given the proper degree of dedication to the object of our efforts.

To put this into simpler terms, if one success permanently defined you, there would be no reason to keep improving. At the same time, if one failure truly ended your chances, no one would ever recover from mistakes. But that’s obviously not how things work. Progress is built on repetition, adjustment, and persistence, not perfection. The people we tend to view as “successful” are rarely the ones who got it right the first time, they’re more often the ones who kept going after getting it wrong multiple times.

This idea also forces a shift in mindset. Instead of treating success as a finish line, it becomes more like a checkpoint. You reach it, acknowledge it, and then move forward. Likewise, failure stops being something to avoid at all costs and instead becomes something useful. It shows what doesn’t work, which is just as important as finding what does.

For example, for an extended period of time, I had a really hard time keeping up in my AP World History class due to repeatedly scoring low on the MCQ assessments. However, instead of throwing in the towel and settling for a C each time, believing it's just what I’m bound to score and will have to deal with the grade drop, I progressively started putting more and more time into the focus I spent on these types of questions. I didn’t immediately start getting A’s, but by the end of the year I had a low A in the class. In a situation such as this, the outcome wasn’t determined by one moment, but by whether or not I kept putting in effort afterward. That’s exactly what Churchill is getting at.

Ultimately, the quote emphasizes consistency over outcome. Courage, in this case, isn’t some dramatic or heroic act, it’s simply choosing not to quit. It’s continuing to show up even when results aren’t immediate or obvious. And in the long run, that mindset is what separates temporary results from real, lasting growth.
